Bringing your medication into Singapore, the right way

For most travellers, personal medication is a non-issue. For a specific set of common prescriptions, it needs paperwork before you fly — and finding out at the airport is the wrong time.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Singapore regulates medicines tightly, and a handful of very common prescriptions fall under controlled-substance rules. This is one of the most anxiety-generating and worst-covered topics for travellers — here's the clean version.

Generally fine (with basic sense)

  • Ordinary prescription meds (blood pressure, cholesterol, antibiotics, contraceptives, asthma inhalers) for personal use — typically up to three months' supply.
  • Keep them in original packaging, and carry your prescription or a doctor's letter naming you, the drug and the dosage.
  • Common OTC items (paracetamol, antihistamines) — no issue; most are freely available here anyway.

Needs advance approval from HSA

Medicines containing controlled substances commonly require you to apply to the Health Sciences Authority before arrival. The usual suspects:

  • Sleeping pills and anti-anxiety meds (benzodiazepines — e.g. diazepam, alprazolam)
  • ADHD medication (stimulants — e.g. methylphenidate, amphetamine-based)
  • Strong painkillers (opioids — e.g. morphine, oxycodone, tramadol in some forms)
  • Codeine-containing painkillers and cough syrups

The approval is a form-based application to HSA made in advance (allow at least 10 working days), stating the medicine, quantity and your travel dates. It's routine — but it must exist before you land with the drugs.

Absolute no

Cannabis and CBD products — including "legal at home" edibles, oils and vape cartridges — are controlled drugs in Singapore. Do not bring them in any form. See our drug-laws guide for why this is the one rule to take absolutely literally.

The pre-flight checklist

  • List your meds → check each against HSA's controlled list (hsa.gov.sg).
  • Anything controlled → apply to HSA in advance; carry the approval with the meds.
  • Everything → original packaging + prescription/doctor's letter, in your carry-on.
  • Longer stays: most common medicines are available locally by seeing a GP — often easier than importing repeat supplies.
