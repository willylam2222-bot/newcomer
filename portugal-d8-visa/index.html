The Portugal D8 visa: Europe's nomad gateway, decoded

The D8 turned Portugal into remote work's front door to Europe. The requirements are clear-ish, the process is bureaucratic, and the payoff — EU residence — is real.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Since 2022, Portugal's D8 visa has been the structured way for non-EU remote workers to live in Portugal legally — and, on the residency track, to build toward permanent EU residence. Here's the current shape without the relocation-agency gloss.

The core requirements

  • Remote income ≥ 4× the Portuguese minimum wage — as of recent rules roughly €3,500/month, from employment or freelancing for entities outside Portugal. Contracts, invoices and bank statements prove it.
  • Savings — typically around 12× the minimum wage (~€10,000+) held in your name, sometimes more per dependant.
  • A NIF (Portuguese tax number) and a Portuguese bank account — both obtainable remotely, both needed early (see our NIF guide).
  • Clean criminal record, proof of accommodation for the initial period, health insurance to start.

Two tracks — pick deliberately

  • Temporary-stay track: up to a year, renewable — for trying Portugal without committing. Simpler, but doesn't build toward residence.
  • Residency track (the popular one): a 4-month entry visa → residence permit (~2 years, renewable) → after 5 years' legal residence, eligibility for permanent residence and (rules permitting — citizenship legislation has been under active political revision) naturalisation. This 5-year horizon is why people choose Portugal over prettier tax deals elsewhere.

What the process actually feels like

  • Apply at the Portuguese consulate serving your residence, not from inside Portugal (rules on switching status exist but the consular route is the clean one).
  • Consular processing runs weeks-to-months and varies wildly by consulate; the in-country immigration agency (AIMA) inherited a serious appointment backlog — patience is a requirement, not a virtue, here.
  • Most applicants use a lawyer or service (€500–1,500); it's not mandatory, but the paperwork choreography (NIF → bank → visa → AIMA appointment) rewards someone who's done it before.
Tax honesty

The famous NHR tax regime closed to new entrants in 2024; its narrower successor (for specific high-value professions) covers far fewer people. Assume normal Portuguese progressive tax rates unless a professional confirms you qualify for the new regime — plan the move on lifestyle maths, not tax fantasy.

Who the D8 fits

Remote employees and freelancers clearing the income bar who want Europe with a residence path — not just a season abroad. If you only want winter sun, the temporary track (or plain Schengen tourism) is less paperwork; if you want the EU foothold, the D8 residency track remains one of the most achievable doors in.
