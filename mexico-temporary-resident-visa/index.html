Staying in Mexico legally: the temporary resident visa, explained

Mexico gives tourists a generous stay and offers one of the world's more achievable residency routes for people with steady income — but with a catch about where you apply.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Mexico has been a remote-work magnet partly because staying is comparatively easy — for those who plan it right. The menu:

Short stays: the tourist entry

  • Most Western passports get a tourist entry of up to 180 days — but the days are now discretionary. Border officers increasingly grant less than the old automatic six months (30, 60, 90 days happen), especially to obvious repeat visitors. Don't assume 180; you get what's stamped.
  • This discretion quietly ended the "live in Mexico forever on tourist stamps" era for many — the residency route below is the stable answer.

The Temporary Resident Visa (the nomad favorite)

A renewable residency (1 year, extendable up to 4) for people who can show economic solvency. The thresholds are set in Mexican minimum-wage multiples and move yearly, but in practice mean roughly:

  • Income route: ~$4,300+ USD/month in after-tax income over the last ~6 months, or
  • Savings route: ~$70,000+ USD average balance over the last ~12 months.
  • (Exact figures vary by consulate and the peso/minimum-wage math — verify current numbers with your specific consulate.)
The catch: you must apply from ABROAD

The temporary resident visa is applied for at a Mexican consulate outside Mexico — you cannot switch from a tourist stamp to residency from inside the country (with narrow exceptions). Plan to book a consulate appointment in your home country (or a third country) before you're settled in. After consulate approval you enter Mexico and complete the process at INM within 30 days to get your resident card. This trips up people who "fall in love with CDMX and decide to stay" — the paperwork wants you to start from outside.

Paths onward

  • Permanent residency — after 4 years as a temporary resident, or directly with higher financial thresholds (or through family/retirement).
  • Working locally needs a temporary resident visa with work permission tied to a Mexican employer; the standard financial-solvency version covers foreign-sourced income only.
  • Retirement and family unity (spouse/children of Mexicans or residents) are separate, often easier routes.

Practical notes

  • Consulate requirements and appointment availability vary enormously — some are booked out months ahead. Start early.
  • Many applicants use a facilitator, but the process is doable solo if you read your consulate's specific checklist carefully.
  • Bring originals and copies of everything; consulates are strict on documentation and bank-statement formatting.
