Is Mexico City safe? The nuanced truth headlines skip

The word 'Mexico' triggers cartel headlines; the reality of Mexico City for the neighborhoods newcomers live in is closer to a big European capital with a pickpocket problem.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

No topic is more distorted for Mexico than safety. National cartel violence is real but geographically concentrated in specific states and border regions — not in the leafy CDMX neighborhoods where expats and tourists spend their time. Judging Mexico City by Sinaloa headlines is like judging Chicago by a war zone on another continent. Here's the honest, local picture.

The geography of safety (this is everything)

  • Where visitors and expats actually are — Roma, Condesa, Polanco, Coyoacán, Juárez, Del Valle — are walkable, cafe-dense, and safe day and night with normal city awareness. This is where the "CDMX is wonderful" reviews come from, and they're accurate.
  • The city has areas to avoid, as every megacity does — particular outer boroughs and certain spots after dark. Locals will tell you which; the tourist zones aren't among them.
  • Mexico's travel-advisory levels are state-by-state for a reason. Mexico City sits well below the "do not travel" border states. Read the advisory by state, not the country headline.

The real risks (ranked honestly)

  • Petty crime & pickpocketing — the actual daily risk: phones snatched, bags opened on the crowded Metro, distraction thefts in markets. Crossbody bag on the front, phone away in crowds, and you've handled 90% of it.
  • Taxi risk — use apps. Hailing street taxis is the classic mistake (express-kidnapping stories almost always involve them). Uber and Didi are ubiquitous, cheap and tracked — use them, especially at night. This single habit removes the scariest category.
  • Altitude, not attackers. CDMX sits at 2,240m — newcomers underestimate it. Expect breathlessness, faster drunkenness, and a day or two of adjustment. Hydrate, ease the first days, go easy on alcohol on arrival.
  • Earthquakes — the city is seismically active. Learn the drill, note your building's safety, and don't panic at the loudspeaker alarm (it gives seconds of warning — move to safety calmly).
The one-line safety strategy

Live/stay in the central neighborhoods, take Uber/Didi instead of street taxis, wear your bag crossed on the front, don't flash phones or jewelry, and respect the altitude. Do that and CDMX is one of the world's great, and genuinely safe-feeling, big cities.

For solo and female travelers

Solo travel in the central neighborhoods is common and comfortable; the Metro has women-only cars at rush hour for a reason (groping happens on packed trains, same as Tokyo). General street harassment exists at big-city levels — the apps-not-taxis and central-neighborhoods rules do most of the work. Emergency number: 911 (yes, the same as the US).
