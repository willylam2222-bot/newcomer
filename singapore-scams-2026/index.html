The scams actually running in Singapore right now

Street crime here is rare. Scams are not — they're Singapore's biggest crime category by losses, and newcomers are the softest targets.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Scam losses in Singapore run to hundreds of millions of dollars a year, and the victims aren't gullible — the scripts are professional and localised. Newcomers get hit hardest because you don't yet know what "normal" looks like. Here's the current playbook.

The big four right now

  • Government / bank impersonation. A call or text "from" the police, MAS, DBS, Singpass or a courier: something is wrong with your account/parcel/visa, and you must "verify" or move money to a "safe account." No real agency or bank ever asks this. This is the single most expensive scam category.
  • Rental scams. A "landlord" (conveniently overseas) wants a deposit to hold a unit you haven't viewed. Real listings can be verified: see the unit, verify the agent's CEA number, pay only on signing. Newcomers under time pressure are the prime target.
  • Job scams. "Easy commission tasks" that start with small payouts to you, then require you to top up to unlock earnings. Any job that needs your money first is a scam, always.
  • E-commerce scams. Too-cheap deals on marketplaces; payment via bank transfer to a stranger; goods never arrive. Use platform payments, not direct transfers.
The one habit that defeats most of them

Scams work by manufacturing urgency. The counter-move is boring: hang up, wait, and verify through an official channel you found yourself (the number on the bank's website, not the number that called you). Urgency + money movement = scam, every time.

Tools Singapore gives you

  • ScamShield app — blocks known scam calls/SMS; official checker for suspicious messages.
  • 1799 — the national anti-scam hotline, for checking or reporting.
  • Money Lock — most major banks let you lock a portion of savings so it can't be transferred digitally at all. Excellent default for newcomers.

If you've been scammed

Call your bank immediately to freeze transfers, then file a police report (online or any station) and report via ScamShield. Speed matters more than embarrassment — banks can sometimes claw back funds that haven't left the system.
