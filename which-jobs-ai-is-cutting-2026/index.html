The jobs AI is really cutting in 2026 — with the receipts

More than 150,000 roles were cut in the first half of 2026 with employers pointing at AI. But the story underneath the headlines is more specific — and more useful — than "the robots are coming."

In the first six months of 2026, over 150,000 roles were eliminated by companies that named AI as a factor — roughly 120,000 of them in tech. That's real, and it's accelerating. But "AI is taking jobs" is too blunt to act on. The useful question is which tasks, in which functions, are going first — because that tells you where you actually stand.

The functions being hit first

The cuts aren't random. They cluster exactly where work is repetitive, digital, and low-stakes if a mistake slips through:

  • Customer support. Klarna cut deep as AI absorbed support and operational roles, with its CEO saying AI did the work of hundreds of agents. Salesforce said support cases fell and it stopped backfilling support engineers after deploying AI agents.
  • Back-office and administration. IBM's CEO said AI agents had already replaced hundreds of back-office roles, calling the cuts a direct outcome of automation. Office and administrative support has the highest share of automatable tasks of any category — Goldman Sachs put it around 46%.
  • Operations and routine processing. Banks like ING flagged up to a thousand further roles as digital and AI tools reduced support-function headcount.
  • Content, QA and junior coding. Simple content writing, quality assurance and entry-level coding tasks are increasingly first drafts done by AI and finished by a human — which means fewer humans.

Who's most exposed — the pattern

Across every company statement, the exposed work shares four traits: it's repetitive, mostly cognitive and digital (no physical presence needed), low-stakes if wrong, and doesn't require licensed judgment or accountability. Two groups feel it first:

  • Junior and entry-level workers. Stanford's Digital Economy Lab found a roughly 13% relative drop in employment for 22–25-year-olds in the most AI-exposed occupations — the rungs that used to be "learn on the job" are the easiest to automate.
  • Task-doers rather than decision-owners. If your value is producing a standard output to a brief, that output is exactly what AI produces cheapest.
Where the headlines overstate it
Not every "AI layoff" is really about AI. Some companies use it as a tidy public reason for cuts driven by over-hiring or a soft economy. And "high task exposure" is not the same as "job eliminated" — most roles are being reshaped, with the automatable parts stripped out and the human parts left. Read the claims with that filter.

What this means for you

If your daily work lives in the exposed zone, the answer isn't panic — it's to move deliberately toward the parts of your job that need judgment, accountability or human trust, and to become the person who operates AI rather than the one whose tasks it absorbs. That single shift — from doing the task to directing the tool — is the clearest hedge available in 2026.
