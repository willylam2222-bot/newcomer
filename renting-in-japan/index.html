Renting in Japan: the system nobody warns you about

The rent looks reasonable. Then the move-in invoice arrives at four to six times that number, including a 'gratitude payment' you never get back. Here's the system, decoded.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

Japanese renting runs on a set of fees with no Western equivalent, plus a guarantor system that assumes you have a Japanese employer or family. It's navigable — hundreds of thousands of foreigners rent happily — but only once someone explains the invoice.

The move-in invoice, itemised

ItemTypicalWhat it is
Deposit (shikikin)1–2 monthsRefundable-ish (cleaning deducted)
Key money (reikin)0–2 monthsA non-refundable "thank you" to the landlord. Yes, really.
Agency fee~1 month + taxTo the realtor
Guarantor company~0.5–1 month + annual renewalStands in for the family guarantor you don't have
First month + insurance + lock change~1.5 monthsAssorted onboarding

Total: commonly 4–6 months' rent in cash before you get keys. On a ¥120,000 Tokyo one-room, that's ¥500,000–700,000.

The guarantor wall (and how foreigners clear it)

Nearly all leases require a guarantor. Foreigners without Japanese family use a guarantor company (hoshou-gaisha) — most landlords accept specific ones, your agency arranges it, and the fee above covers it. What you'll need: residence card, proof of income/employment (or university enrolment), a Japanese phone number, and often a Japanese bank account — which creates a chicken-and-egg week; short-term housing first solves it.

Routes that skip the worst

  • UR housing — public-ish apartments with no key money, no agency fee, no guarantor. Income requirements apply and locations are further out, but for families especially it's the best-kept non-secret in Japanese renting.
  • Foreigner-friendly agencies & guesthouses — share houses and furnished apartments (monthly mansions) with simple contracts; pricier per month, but near-zero move-in cost. The standard first-6-months play.
  • Zero-reikin listings — increasingly common; filter for them. Sometimes the "free" is priced into rent, so compare totals over your expected stay.
Three expectations to set

Apartments come completely unfurnished (often no light fixtures or curtain rails); some landlords still decline foreign applicants (agencies know who won't — let them route you); and paying via bank transfer with a Japanese account is the norm, not cards.
