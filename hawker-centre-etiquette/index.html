Hawker centres: the unwritten rules, written down

The tissue packet on that empty table isn't litter. It's a legally binding reservation — culturally speaking.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

Hawker centres are Singapore's living room: Michelin-listed food at S$4–6, shared tables, organised chaos. The etiquette is simple once someone explains it. Consider this that explanation.

Chope: the tissue-packet reservation

To "chope" is to reserve a seat — traditionally by placing a packet of tissues (or an umbrella, or a staff pass) on the table before going to queue for food. A choped table is taken. Respect it, and feel free to chope your own; it's universally understood.

Ordering: the rhythm

  • Queue at the stall, order, and either wait or give your table number — stalls with "self-collection" signs mean wait; others deliver.
  • Pay per stall (cash or PayNow at many — see our cash guide). Drinks come from a separate drinks stall.
  • "Makan here or tapao?" — eating in, or takeaway (tapao = pack to go). Two words worth knowing.

Tray returns: the rule with teeth

Return your tray

Since 2021, leaving your tray and litter on the table is an enforceable offence — warnings first, fines for repeat cases. Tray-return stations are everywhere (separated by halal / non-halal at some centres — match your tray to the right rack).

Kopi without fear

Coffee-stall vocabulary, decoded: kopi = coffee with condensed milk; kopi-o = black with sugar; kopi-c = with evaporated milk and sugar; add kosong for no sugar (kopi-o kosong = black, no sugar); peng = iced (kopi peng). Same system for teh (tea). Order once and you're a regular.

Small courtesies

  • Address older stallholders as "uncle" or "auntie" — friendly, normal, appreciated.
  • Shared tables are normal at peak hours; asking "can I sit?" with a gesture is all it takes.
  • Clear your own tissue/litter even where cleaners exist. It's the culture now.
