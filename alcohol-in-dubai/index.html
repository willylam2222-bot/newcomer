Drinking in Dubai: exactly how it works now

Dubai has bars, brunches and beach clubs — and also arrests tourists over alcohol every year. The difference is entirely about where and how drunk.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Alcohol in Dubai is legal, regulated and widely available to non-Muslim visitors aged 21+. The rules have been progressively liberalised — but the boundaries that remain are enforced without much humour. Here's the current shape.

Where drinking is legal

  • Licensed venues — hotel bars and restaurants, standalone licensed restaurants, clubs, beach clubs. This is where Dubai's famous drinking culture lives, and as a tourist you just order.
  • Licensed retail — liquor stores sell to visitors (bring your passport). Recent reforms scrapped the old alcohol tax and made personal licences free, making retail buying far simpler than the old horror stories suggest.
  • Your hotel room / private residence — drinking legally bought alcohol privately is fine.

Where it stays illegal

  • Any public place — streets, public beaches, parks, cars, taxis. No open containers, ever.
  • Being drunk in public. This is the big one: the offence isn't the beer at the bar, it's being visibly intoxicated outside it. Most alcohol-related tourist arrests are for drunken behaviour — airport incidents especially.
  • Under 21, and buying for anyone under 21.
  • Drink-driving: zero tolerance. Not "one drink is okay" — zero. Taxis and Careem are everywhere; use them.
The operating rule

Drink where a licence hangs on the wall, take a taxi home, and arrive at the airport sober. Those three habits eliminate essentially all tourist alcohol trouble in Dubai.

Beyond Dubai: the emirates differ

  • Abu Dhabi — similar licensed-venue system, slightly more conservative in feel.
  • Sharjah — completely dry. No alcohol at all, including in hotels and your luggage when staying there. If you're day-tripping from Sharjah accommodation, plan around it.
  • Ramadan: licensed venues still serve (typically after sunset historically, now more normally in Dubai) but the public-discretion bar rises across the board.
