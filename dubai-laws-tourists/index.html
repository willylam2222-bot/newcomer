What's actually illegal in Dubai: the calm version

Half the internet says you'll be jailed for holding hands in Dubai; the other half says anything goes. Both are wrong — here's where the real lines are.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

Dubai runs on a simple deal: a hyper-modern, tolerant-in-practice tourist economy layered over a legal system rooted in conservative values. Millions of visitors have effortless trips because the genuinely enforced rules are few and avoidable. The problem is that online content either fear-mongers or hand-waves. Here's the sober middle.

The rules that genuinely catch tourists

  • Social media & photography. This is the modern trap. Photographing people without consent, posting someone identifiable without permission, sharing accident/crash photos, and swearing at someone online (including in WhatsApp messages) are all offences under UAE cybercrime law — and cases have been brought over exactly these. Photograph buildings, not strangers; vent verbally, not in writing.
  • Drugs — absolute zero tolerance. Trace amounts in your system or luggage are prosecutable. CBD products are illegal (unlike vapes, which are legal here). Check every bag you carry; never transport anything for anyone. Some prescription meds need advance approval — see our UAE medication guide.
  • Drinking outside licensed settings. Alcohol itself is fine for visitors 21+ in licensed venues (see our Dubai alcohol guide) — but drinking or being visibly drunk in public places (streets, beaches, taxis) is an offence, and it's what actually gets tourists arrested, usually after an airport lounge too many.
  • Aggression. Rude gestures, road rage, public shouting matches — offensive behaviour is criminal, not just impolite, and complaints are acted on.

The fears that are outdated or overblown

  • Unmarried couples sharing a hotel room — decriminalised in the 2020–21 legal reforms; hotels don't ask and haven't for years.
  • Holding hands — fine for couples. It's kissing and heavy PDA in public that draws complaints (see our dress & PDA guide).
  • "You can't drink in Dubai" — you can, licensed and 21+, and rules were further liberalised in recent years.
  • Dress police — no such thing; the norm is modest-casual in malls and anything-goes at beach clubs. It's a courtesy code, not a criminal one, outside government buildings and mosques.
The pattern behind all of it

Dubai polices public order and complaints, not private behaviour. What happens discreetly in your hotel is your business; what's loud, public, photographed or posted is where the law engages. Calibrate to that and the city is easy.

Ramadan footnote

During Ramadan, norms tighten: eating, drinking and smoking in public daylight hours draws far more sensitivity (Dubai's restaurants now mostly serve normally, often behind screens), dress codes are observed more carefully, and music/party culture dials down. It's a courtesy month — match it.
