What Mexico City actually costs — and the gentrification asterisk

Mexico City is a bargain from a US salary and increasingly not one from a Mexican salary — the gap between those two truths is the whole story.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

CDMX became North America's remote-work darling because it offers a world-class city at a fraction of US prices. That's still true — with an honest asterisk: the neighborhoods expats love have gentrified fast, and prices there now sit far above the local baseline.

  • The trendy neighborhoods now rent in dollars-equivalent that would surprise you — Airbnb and expat demand pushed them up sharply. One neighborhood over (Del Valle, Narvarte, Escandón) gets you 80% of the charm at 60% of the price.
  • Furnished + short-term commands a big premium; a 12-month local lease (with a fiador/guarantor or deposit-heavy arrangement) is far cheaper if you're staying.

The two-economy reality, on a plate

  • Local lane: tacos al pastor for a couple of dollars, comida corrida (set lunch) $4–6, the Metro at ~$0.30 a ride, a barber $5. Living local, CDMX is genuinely cheap.
  • Expat lane: specialty coffee $4–5, Roma brunch $15–25, imported groceries and craft cocktails at near-US prices. A fully expat lifestyle erases much of the savings — the city will happily sell you a San Francisco cost of living if you let it.

The rest

  • Transport: Metro and Metrobús pennies-cheap; Uber/Didi affordable and the safe default (see our safety guide). A car is unnecessary and parking is misery.
  • Healthcare: excellent private hospitals (Médica Sur, ABC) at a fraction of US costs; private insurance $50–150/month by age. Many expats pay out of pocket for routine care because it's that reasonable.
  • Utilities & internet: modest; fiber is fast and cheap; no heating needed and AC rarely (the altitude keeps it temperate year-round — one of CDMX's underrated perks).
Honest monthly budgets (single person)

~$1,000 USD: non-trendy central neighborhood, local-lane living — comfortable and very local. ~$1,800–2,500: Roma/Condesa furnished, mixed diet, the standard remote-worker CDMX life. ~$3,500+: Polanco and a fully imported lifestyle — lovely, and no longer a bargain. The asterisk: what feels cheap to a dollar-earner is expensive to locals, and the friction that creates is real.
