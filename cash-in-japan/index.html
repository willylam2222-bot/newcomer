Cash in Japan: less essential than the myth, more than you'd think

The 'Japan is cash-only' warning is a decade stale. The 'Japan is cashless now' take is also wrong. The truth has a simple shape.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

Japan's payment landscape modernised fast — cards, IC-card tap and QR pay (PayPay) are everywhere in cities. But a stubborn cash layer remains exactly where tourists spend: small food joints, shrines, festivals, and the countryside. Here's the split.

Where cards/tap always work

  • Konbini (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart), chain restaurants, department stores, hotels, big attractions, taxis in cities, and increasingly train fare gates (contactless credit tap has been rolling out across metros).
  • Your transport IC card (Suica/Pasmo — see our IC card guide) also pays at konbini, vending machines and many casual spots — the most underrated payment method in Japan.

Where you still need yen

  • Small izakaya, ramen shops with ticket machines, old-school restaurants — many take cash only, and ticket-machine shops often take only coins/notes.
  • Shrines and temples — offerings, omamori charms, goshuin stamps: cash.
  • Markets, festivals (matsuri), street food, small-town anything.
  • Some clinics, some guesthouses, coin lockers and laundromats.
The ATM answer

Don't exchange money at home — withdraw yen from 7-Eleven (Seven Bank) or Japan Post ATMs, which reliably accept foreign cards, have English menus, and sit on every other corner. A low-FX-fee travel card (Wise/Revolut-style) makes the rate near-perfect.

How much cash to carry

City trip: ¥10,000–20,000 on you covers days of small-spot eating; top up at konbini ATMs as needed. Rural travel: assume cash-first and carry more. Coins accumulate fast — spend them at vending machines and konbini, or load them onto your IC card at station machines.

The one-line rule

If it's a chain or has a logo: card. If it's delicious, tiny and seats eight people: cash. Plan for both and Japan's payment quirks cost you nothing.
