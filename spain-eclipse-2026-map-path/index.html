The August 12 2026 eclipse path across Spain — town by town

The path of totality on 12 August 2026 is only about 300 km wide. Step outside it and you get a partial eclipse — a completely different, far lesser experience. Here's who's in and who's out.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

The single most important question for the 2026 Spain eclipse is binary: is your spot inside the path of totality, or not? Inside, day turns to night and the corona blazes. A few kilometres outside, you get a partial eclipse — the Sun is never fully covered and it stays daylight. There is no 'almost'.

Cities IN the path of totality

  • A Coruña and much of Galicia (Atlantic northwest — where the path enters Spain)
  • Oviedo and León (near the centre-line — longest totality, ~1m48s)
  • Palencia, Burgos (Castile y León — flat terrain, good clear-sky odds)
  • Zaragoza (Aragón)
  • Valencia (Mediterranean coast — very low Sun)
  • Palma de Mallorca (Balearics — Sun almost setting, ~2° up, where the path exits)

Cities NOT in the path (partial only)

  • Madrid — just south of the path. Partial eclipse only.
  • Barcelona — just outside the path. Partial eclipse only.
  • Seville, Málaga and the south — a smaller partial eclipse.
Don't plan around Madrid or Barcelona. They're the obvious travel hubs, but both miss totality. If you're flying into either, treat it as a gateway and travel north into the path for the 12th.

The path in one line

Totality sweeps from Galicia in the northwest (arriving ~19:27 local) down through Castile y León and Aragón, and out over Valencia and the Balearics near sunset (~20:35). The band is ~300 km wide where it hits the Atlantic coast and narrows as it crosses.

Being in the path isn't enough

Even inside totality, the Sun is only 2–12° above the horizon — so you also need a clear western-northwestern horizon and, ideally, good local weather. Which town, which viewpoint and which clear-sky odds all matter, and that's where the playbook below earns its keep.
