The real cost of living in Singapore

Singapore is expensive in three places — housing, cars and alcohol — and surprisingly affordable in one that saves you daily: food.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

Singapore regularly tops "most expensive city" lists, but the headline hides how the costs actually fall. Two things dominate any budget — housing and, if you're mad enough to want one, a car. Almost everything else is manageable, and everyday food is a genuine bargain.

Housing — the biggest line by far

Rent is the number that shapes your whole budget. Indicative monthly bands (they move with location, condition and the market cycle):

TypeRough monthly (S$)
Common room in a shared flat~1,000 – 1,800
Whole HDB flat (3–4 rooms)~2,800 – 4,200
1-bedroom condo~3,000 – 4,500
2-bedroom condo~4,200 – 6,500+

The single biggest lever for a newcomer is whole-unit vs a room: renting a common room can cost half or less of a whole unit. Budget roughly two months' rent upfront (deposit + first month) before you get keys.

Food — where Singapore is cheap

Hawker centres are the secret to living well cheaply. A cooked meal at a hawker stall runs a few dollars; a kopi (coffee) is around a dollar or two. Cook or eat at hawkers and your food budget stays low. Eat at restaurants and drink imported alcohol, and it climbs fast — a beer at a bar can cost more than a hawker meal.

Getting around — genuinely affordable

Public transport is excellent and cheap: most MRT/bus trips cost a couple of dollars, and you just tap a contactless card or phone (SimplyGo). A car, by contrast, is a luxury — you pay for a Certificate of Entitlement (COE) that can cost more than the car itself, plus taxes. Most residents don't own one, and don't need to.

The rest

  • Utilities: electricity/water/gas for a flat commonly run a few hundred dollars a month, more with heavy aircon use.
  • Mobile & internet: competitive and reasonable; prepaid SIMs are cheap.
  • Healthcare: high quality; budget for insurance, as out-of-pocket costs at private clinics/hospitals add up.
  • Alcohol & tobacco: heavily taxed and expensive.
Where newcomers overspend

Signing for a whole unit when a room would do, eating out instead of hawker food, and drinking imported alcohol at bars. Fix those three and Singapore becomes far more affordable than its reputation.

Bottom line

Your Singapore budget is really a housing decision plus a lifestyle decision. Get the rent right — the right size unit, the right district, the right lease — and the rest is comfortable. That first rental choice is where the money is won or lost.
