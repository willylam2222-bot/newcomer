AI for the job hunt: what actually helps (and what gets you caught)

AI can cut your job-search time in half — or fill your applications with the generic sludge every recruiter now recognises on sight. The difference is method.

Job searching is a perfect AI task: repetitive, high-stakes, and full of writing you'd rather not do from scratch. But recruiters read a hundred AI-generated cover letters a week and bin them on the second cliché. Here's how to use AI to win, not to blend in.

The CV: tailor, don't fabricate

Here's my CV [paste] and this job posting [paste]. Rewrite my top 6 bullet points to mirror the posting's language and priorities — honestly, using only things I actually did. Then list the 3 biggest gaps between me and this role so I can address them.
  • Mirror the keywords — many applications pass through automated screening (ATS) that matches your words to the posting. AI is excellent at surfacing which of your real experiences to foreground.
  • Never invent — a fabricated skill surfaces in the interview and ends the process. AI's job is re-framing truth, not manufacturing it.

Cover letters that don't scream "AI"

The tell-tale AI cover letter is generic, over-formal, and full of "I am excited to leverage my synergies". Kill that with three inputs:

Write a cover letter for [role] at [company]. About me: [3 real facts]. Why this company specifically: [something genuine you found]. Match my voice — here's how I actually write: [paste a paragraph you wrote]. Rules: under 250 words, no clichés (no "team player", "passionate", "leverage"), open with something other than "I am writing to apply".

The voice sample is the secret. Without it, AI writes like AI. With it, AI writes like a sharper version of you.

Interview prep: the roleplay nobody uses

  • Mock interviewer: "You're the hiring manager for [role]. Interview me — one question at a time, follow up on weak answers, and afterward critique my responses for substance and confidence."
  • The dreaded questions: "Given my background [paste], what are the 5 hardest questions I'll get, including the gaps in my CV? Give me a strong, honest answer framework for each."
  • Company research: "Summarise what I should know about [company] before interviewing: their business, recent news, likely priorities, and 3 smart questions I could ask that show I did homework." (Verify the facts — see verification note below.)
The two mistakes that get you caught

1) Submitting unread AI output — hallucinated companies, wrong role titles, "[insert company here]" left in. Always read every word aloud before sending. 2) Letting AI flatten your voice — recruiters hire people, not prose. Use AI to draft and sharpen; keep the parts that sound like you.

The workflow that compounds

Build one strong "master" CV and voice sample, then reuse them across every application with the tailoring prompts above — 20 minutes per application instead of two hours, at higher quality.
