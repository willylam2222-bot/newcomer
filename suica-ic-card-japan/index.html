The IC card question, answered for 2026

One tap card runs all of urban Japan — trains, subways, buses, konbini, vending machines. The only real question in 2026 is which version of it you set up.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

Japan's IC cards (Suica, Pasmo, ICOCA and regional siblings) are interoperable: one card taps through virtually every city's gates and pays at convenience stores, vending machines and lockers. After the chip-shortage chaos of past years, availability is normal again — and you have three good options.

Option 1: Mobile Suica (best for iPhone users)

Add Suica directly in Apple Wallet (Settings → Wallet → Add Transit Card) — no app, no Japanese needed, works instantly, and tops up from Apple Pay anywhere. Your phone or watch becomes the tap. This is the best option for most visitors with iPhones. (Android: mobile Suica generally requires a Japanese-market device — most foreign Android phones can't; get a physical card instead.)

Option 2: A physical card (Welcome Suica / Pasmo Passport)

Sold at airports and major stations: tourist versions (like Welcome Suica, valid 28 days, no deposit) or regular cards (small refundable deposit). Top up with cash at any station machine. Get one per traveller — kids' versions exist with child fares.

Option 3: Just tap your credit card

Contactless credit-card entry has been rolling out across metros and railways nationwide — many gates now take a foreign Visa/Mastercard tap directly. Coverage is broad but not yet universal (notably, some JR East lines lag), so treat it as a convenient backup rather than your only method for now.

What IC cards don't cover

Long-distance shinkansen and limited-express trains need separate tickets (or a JR Pass if your itinerary justifies one — do the math, it no longer auto-wins after the price rises). IC cards are for city transit and small payments.

Practical notes

  • Top up with cash at machines (English menus) or via Apple Pay for Mobile Suica; card balance caps at ¥20,000.
  • The same tap pays at konbini, vending machines, coin lockers and many casual restaurants — it quietly solves half of Japan's cash quirks.
  • Keep ¥1,000–2,000 balance for the airport train home; refund physical deposits at station counters before leaving (Welcome Suica has no deposit to refund).
