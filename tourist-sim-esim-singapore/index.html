Getting connected in Singapore: SIM, eSIM, or nothing?

Short answer: buy an eSIM before you fly or a tourist SIM at Changi — both are cheap. The interesting question is which one your situation actually needs.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

Singapore has excellent, cheap mobile coverage from three main operators (Singtel, StarHub, M1) plus budget brands riding their networks. Getting connected takes minutes — here's the decision tree.

Tourists (days to weeks)

  • eSIM (buy before you fly). If your phone supports eSIM, buying a Singapore or regional data eSIM online before departure means you land connected. Usually the least friction; data-only is fine since messaging runs on WhatsApp/Telegram anyway.
  • Tourist SIM at Changi. Physical SIMs from the operators' airport counters and convenience stores come loaded with generous data (often 100GB-class for S$12–30). Bring your passport — registration is mandatory and takes minutes.
  • Just roaming. If your home plan includes affordable roaming, Singapore's short-trip case is honestly fine on it — the city is blanketed in Wi-Fi too.

New residents (months+)

Skip tourist products and go straight to a local prepaid or SIM-only postpaid plan. SIM-only monthly plans are cheap and generous, no contract needed. You'll need your passport (and later your pass/FIN). Keep the number — it becomes your PayNow identity, your bank OTP channel, and effectively your local ID for daily life.

The one thing that matters more than price

Whatever you buy, your Singapore mobile number is the key that unlocks PayNow and bank verification once you're a resident. Choose an operator you'll keep, register it properly under your own passport, and guard it like a bank card.

Practical notes

  • All SIM registration is passport-based and enforced; there's a cap on prepaid SIMs per identity.
  • Coverage is effectively universal, including the MRT tunnels — buy on price and data, not "network quality" marketing.
  • Wi-Fi: hotels, malls and cafés are well covered; the free public network exists but signing up is more hassle than a cheap eSIM.
