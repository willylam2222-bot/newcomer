The NIF: Portugal's everything-number, and how to get yours

Portugal has one bureaucratic key that unlocks all the other doors. Get the NIF first — ideally before your flight — and every other step gets easier.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

The NIF (Número de Identificação Fiscal) is Portugal's tax identification number — and in practice its universal ID for economic life. You need it to rent a flat, open a bank account, get a phone contract, set up utilities, buy property, even claim invoices at the pharmacy. It's step zero of any Portuguese plan, including the D8 visa (see our guide).

How to get one

  • Remotely, before arriving (the smart way): online NIF services and Portuguese lawyers obtain it with a passport scan and proof of address, typically in days for €50–150. For visa applicants this is the standard first move, since the visa file itself wants the NIF.
  • In person at Finanças: free at a tax office (or some Citizen Shops) — bring passport and proof of address. The catch is appointment availability and queues in the big cities; it's the budget route if you're already in Portugal with time.

The fiscal representative question

Non-EU/EEA applicants living outside Portugal have traditionally needed a fiscal representative — a Portugal-based person or firm who receives tax correspondence for you. Recent rules softened this (electronic notifications can substitute in many cases), but in practice most remote NIF services bundle representation for the first year, then you drop it once you're resident with a Portuguese address. Budget for it as part of the setup cost rather than fighting it.

After the NIF: the setup chain

  1. NIF (remote, days)
  2. Portuguese bank account — several banks and fintechs open accounts for non-residents with a NIF + passport; needed for the visa's proof-of-funds and later for rent.
  3. Visa application (D8 or other) at your consulate.
  4. Arrival → lease, utilities, SNS number, NISS (social security) if working — each asks for the NIF, which by now you'll recite from memory.
Two practical notes

Give your NIF at shop checkouts ("contribuinte?") only if you want purchases linked for tax deductions — it's optional, and as a non-resident you can simply say no. And guard the number as you would any national ID: it's requested often, but only institutions actually need it.
