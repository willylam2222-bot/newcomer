AI as your personal tutor: the method that actually works

The worst way to learn with AI is the most common: asking for explanations and nodding along. The best way makes the AI ask YOU the questions.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

AI is the first infinitely patient, always-available tutor in history — but a tutor you only listen to teaches you almost nothing. Learning science is unambiguous: retrieval beats rereading. The prompts below force the retrieval.

1. Start with a syllabus, not a question

I want to learn [topic] to the level where I can [concrete goal]. My background: [honest]. Build me a syllabus: 6–10 milestones in dependency order, each with what I should be able to DO to prove I've got it.

This turns a vague ambition into a checkable path — and "what I can do" beats "what I've read" as a progress measure.

2. The Feynman loop (the core move)

Teach me [milestone] in 3 levels: child, smart beginner, practitioner. After each level, stop — I'll explain it back in my own words. Correct my gaps and misconceptions bluntly before moving up a level.

Explaining back is where learning happens; the AI's job is catching the gaps between what you think you said and what's true. Two rounds of this beats ten explanations.

3. Make it quiz you (and keep quizzing)

Quiz me on everything we've covered on [topic]: 5 questions, one at a time, mixing recall and application. Grade honestly, explain what I missed, and note which areas to re-test me on next session.
  • Start each new session with "re-test me on my weak areas from last time" — poor man's spaced repetition, and it works.
  • Ask for application problems ("give me a realistic scenario where I must use this"), not just definitions.

4. Guard against the two failure modes

  • Passive scrolling in disguise: if a session ends and you never typed an explanation or answer, you consumed content — you didn't learn. The ratio to hold: at least half the session is you producing.
  • Confident nonsense: for factual/technical domains, spot-check load-bearing claims against a real source — especially numbers, dates, syntax and anything that changed recently. (Our handbook's verification chapter covers the cheap checking habits.)
The 30-minute daily shape that compounds

5 min: re-test on last session's weak spots → 15 min: Feynman loop on the next milestone → 10 min: application problem. Repeat daily and a syllabus that "would take a course" falls in weeks — the tutor never gets tired, only you do.
