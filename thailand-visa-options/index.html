Staying in Thailand legally: the 2026 options

Thailand made short stays easier and serial border-running harder — a deliberate trade. Here's the current menu, matched to how long you actually want to stay.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Thai immigration modernised significantly in 2024–25: longer visa-free stays for tourists, a genuine long-stay visa for remote workers, and visibly less patience for the old hack of living here on back-to-back tourist entries. The menu now:

Just visiting (up to ~2–3 months)

  • Visa exemption: 60 days on arrival for most Western and many Asian passports (extended from the old 30), plus a 30-day extension at an immigration office (~1,900 baht). That's ~90 days on a stamp — generous by regional standards.
  • Proof of onward travel is technically requestable; airlines enforce it more than immigration does. Have a bookable exit.

Remote workers: the DTV

The Destination Thailand Visa (launched 2024) is the headline: 5-year multi-entry, each stay up to 180 days (extendable once), for remote employees/freelancers with foreign income — plus a "soft power" track (Muay Thai, cooking courses, medical stays). Requirements centre on ~500,000 baht in funds and proof of remote work. It legalised the lifestyle a generation of nomads improvised — at the cost of actually qualifying.

Longer-term anchors

  • Retirement (O/O-A): 50+, ~800,000 baht in a Thai bank or monthly income equivalent — the classic route, annually renewable.
  • Education visas: language schools remain a legitimate route if you actually attend — enforcement against fake enrolment has real teeth now.
  • Work in Thailand = Thai work permit via an employer; the DTV covers foreign-sourced work only, not local employment.
  • LTR (Long-Term Resident): 10-year visa for the wealthy/high-earning — high bars, real benefits (tax perks, fast lanes).
The border-run reality check

Serial visa-exempt entries get flagged: land borders cap entries per year, airport officers question repeat patterns, and denials happen. If you're spending more than half your year here, immigration expects you on a real visa (DTV, retirement, education) — the improvised era is closing, by design.
