Singapore's laws and fines newcomers get wrong

Singapore is orderly because the rules are enforced. Most are common sense — but a few catch newcomers out, and a couple are matters of life and death.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

Singapore's reputation as a "fine city" is a pun locals enjoy — many everyday behaviours that are ignored elsewhere carry actual fines here. The upside is a clean, orderly, low-friction city. The downside is that a habit from home can cost you. Here's what newcomers most often get wrong.

Everyday rules with real fines

BehaviourThe rule
LitteringIllegal and fined; repeat offenders can get Corrective Work Orders (cleaning in public in a vest).
JaywalkingCrossing away from a designated crossing or against the light is an offence and can be fined.
Eating or drinking on the MRTFined — including water. The whole rail system is no-food, no-drink.
Not flushing a public toiletGenuinely an offence that can be fined (rarely enforced, but real).
Smoking outside permitted areasFined. Smoking is banned in many public and covered areas; look for yellow demarcated smoking boxes.
Connecting to someone's Wi-Fi without permissionTreated as an offence under computer-misuse law.

Chewing gum — the famous one

You cannot sell or import chewing gum in Singapore (with narrow exceptions for therapeutic/dental gum from a pharmacist). Chewing gum you brought for personal use isn't going to get you arrested, but you won't find it in shops, and spitting it out in public is a litter offence.

Vaping — illegal, and much harsher since 2026

Don't bring a vape

The purchase, use, possession and import of vapes is prohibited. In 2026 the penalties were sharply increased (fines widely reported up to S$10,000) and vapes containing etomidate ("Kpods") are now treated as a drug offence. Do not pack a vape in your luggage. See our full guide: vaping in Singapore & the 2026 law.

The deadly-serious ones

  • Drugs. Singapore has some of the world's toughest drug laws. Trafficking above certain amounts carries the death penalty; consumption and possession bring heavy penalties, and you can be prosecuted for drugs consumed before you arrived. There is zero tolerance — do not take any chance.
  • Overstaying / working on the wrong pass. Immigration offences are punished seriously, including caning for significant overstays. Match your activity to your visa.
  • Vandalism. Graffiti and defacement can carry fines, jail and caning.

How to stay out of trouble

Ninety percent of it is intuitive: don't litter, cross at crossings, keep food off the trains, don't smoke outside marked areas, and treat anything drug-related as completely off-limits. Do that and Singapore's strict rules simply give you a clean, calm, predictable city.
