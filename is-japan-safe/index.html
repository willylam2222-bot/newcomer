Is Japan safe? Yes — but the real risks aren't crime

Your wallet, left on a café table, will probably be handed to the police. The ground, however, occasionally moves. Japan's safety story is unusual — here's the real shape of it.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

By any crime metric, Japan is extraordinarily safe: violent crime is rare, theft is rare, children commute alone, and lost property routinely comes back via the koban (police box) system. Solo travellers — including women — report some of the most relaxed travel anywhere. So the honest safety conversation is mostly about other things.

The real risks: nature

  • Earthquakes. Japan has small tremors constantly and serious quakes occasionally. Buildings are engineered for them — the correct response indoors is drop/cover away from glass, not running outside. Download NHK World or Yurekuru/Safety Tips app for English alerts; if you're coastal and feel a long strong quake, move uphill immediately without waiting for tsunami sirens.
  • Typhoons (roughly August–October) — mostly a transport problem: trains and flights pre-emptively shut down. Track forecasts, build buffer days, don't fight it.
  • Summer heat — humid 35°C+ waves genuinely hospitalise people; hydrate and pace like locals do.

The few human risks

  • Nightlife district touts — the one genuine scam zone: street touts in Kabukicho (Tokyo) and similar districts luring visitors to bars that produce astronomical bills. The rule is absolute: never follow a street tout into a bar. Choose your own venues and Japan's nightlife is otherwise very safe.
  • Crowded-train groping (chikan) — a known problem on packed commuter trains; women-only cars exist at rush hours and are clearly marked.
  • Bicycles on sidewalks are statistically your most likely daily hazard. Genuinely.
The lost-and-found superpower

Lose something? Go to the nearest koban (police box) or the station's lost-property office — recovery rates in Japan astonish visitors, including for cash. It's the safety culture's best party trick and it works.

Emergency basics

Police 110, ambulance/fire 119. Hospitals with English exist in cities (your embassy keeps lists). Travel insurance still matters — Japanese healthcare is excellent but not free for visitors.
