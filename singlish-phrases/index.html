Singlish, decoded: what everyone around you is saying

Singlish isn't broken English — it's a fast, funny, ruthlessly efficient local layer on top of it. You don't need to speak it. You do need to understand it.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

English is Singapore's working language, so you'll never be lost. But everyday speech runs on Singlish — English compressed and remixed with Malay, Hokkien, Cantonese and Tamil. Here's the working vocabulary, in rough order of how soon you'll meet it.

The particles (the music of Singlish)

  • lah — emphasis/finality: "Okay lah" (fine, settled). The most famous, used everywhere.
  • leh — softens: "Quite expensive leh" (don't you think?).
  • lor — resigned acceptance: "No choice lor."
  • sia — exclamation: "So hot sia!"

The power words

  • Can — the universal yes. "Can you deliver tomorrow?" "Can." Also "Can can" (definitely) and "Cannot" (no). Entire negotiations happen in cans.
  • Chope — to reserve (the tissue packet on the table — see our hawker guide).
  • Makan — food/to eat (Malay). "Makan already?" = have you eaten — which is also how people say hello.
  • Tapao / dabao — takeaway. "Tapao one chicken rice."
  • Shiok — deeply satisfying. Cold kopi peng on a hot day: shiok.
  • Paiseh — embarrassed/sorry-to-trouble-you. "Paiseh, can I squeeze past?"
  • Kiasu — "afraid to lose out"; the national self-deprecation for queueing early, over-preparing, grabbing every deal.
  • Jialat — things are bad. "Traffic jialat today."
  • Uncle / Auntie — respectful-friendly address for older folks (hawkers, taxi drivers). Not rude; expected.
Should you speak it?

Understand everything; deploy sparingly. A well-placed "can" or "paiseh" lands well. A tourist performing "lah" in every sentence lands like a costume. Locals switch fluidly to standard English with you anyway — Singlish fluency is earned by time, not imitation.

Hear it in the wild

"Auntie, one kopi-o kosong, tapao. Can?" — "Can lah!" Congratulations: you now understand 80% of hawker-centre transactions.
