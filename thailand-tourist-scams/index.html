The Thailand scam playbook — and how to walk past it

Thailand's scams are famous precisely because the country is so easy to love — relaxed guard is the raw material. The scripts barely change; knowing them is 90% of immunity.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Thailand is not dangerous — but it hosts some of the world's most polished tourist scams, refined over decades on high-trust visitors. Here's the working repertoire.

The classics (still running daily)

  • "The temple is closed today." A friendly stranger near a major sight redirects you to a tuk-tuk "special tour" ending at a gem shop or tailor. The temple was never closed. Walk to the entrance and see for yourself — this one script has fleeced tourists for 30 years.
  • The gem scam. The endgame of the above: "duty-free" gems to resell at home for profit. They're worthless. Nobody makes money buying gems from a shop a tuk-tuk driver chose.
  • Jet-ski damage claims. Return the jet-ski; operator "finds" pre-existing damage; passport-holding standoff and large cash demand follow. Beat it in advance: video every inch before riding, use operators your hotel vouches for, never leave your passport as deposit.
  • Taxi/tuk-tuk meter games. "Meter broken" or wild flat quotes. Use Grab/Bolt (they've largely killed this problem in cities), or insist on the meter and be willing to step out.
  • Bar bill inflation (and worse, spiked drinks) in nightlife zones. Order by menu with prices, keep tabs cash-per-round, and watch your drink get made.

The newer digital layer

  • Fake accommodation listings for islands in high season — pay only through platforms, never direct transfer to "hold" a room.
  • Rental scooter deposit games — same pattern as jet-skis; same video-everything defence.
  • "Police fine" impersonators — real Thai police issue paper tickets payable at stations; roadside cash "fines" to plainclothes strangers are not a thing you must pay.
The single habit that beats most of it

Unsolicited helpfulness that redirects your plan — a closed temple, a better shop, a special deal ending today — is the opening move of virtually every Thai scam. Warm no, keep walking, follow your original plan. Genuine Thai friendliness (which is everywhere) never needs to redirect you anywhere.

If something goes wrong

The Tourist Police (dial 1155) exist exactly for this, speak English, and take gem/jet-ski/overcharge disputes seriously in tourist zones. For card fraud, your bank first; for theft, a police report same-day for insurance.
