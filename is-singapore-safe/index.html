Is Singapore safe? An honest look at crime and scams

Yes — for violent and street crime, Singapore is among the safest places on earth. The real danger to your money isn't a mugger. It's a scam.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

Singapore consistently ranks at or near the top of global safety indexes. Violent crime is rare, streets are well-lit and monitored, and it's normal to see people walking or taking public transport alone late at night without worry. For most newcomers, the physical safety adjustment is realising you can relax in ways you couldn't back home.

But "safe" doesn't mean "no risk." The threats that actually cost people here are different from what you might expect.

What's genuinely low-risk

  • Street & violent crime — very low. Pickpocketing and mugging are uncommon even in crowded areas.
  • Walking alone at night — generally fine across the island, including for solo travellers and women, though normal common sense still applies.
  • Leaving belongings briefly — locals famously "chope" (reserve) hawker-centre seats with a packet of tissues. Theft is low, but don't treat it as a guarantee for phones and wallets.

The real risk: scams

Scams are the standout crime problem, and losses run into the hundreds of millions a year. Newcomers are prime targets because you don't yet know local norms. The common ones:

  • Rental scams — a fake "landlord" (often claiming to be overseas) asks for a deposit before you view the unit. If you're asked to pay before seeing the actual place, walk away.
  • Government / bank impersonation — calls or texts pretending to be from the police, MAS, a bank, or a delivery company, pushing you to "verify" or move money. Real agencies never ask for that.
  • Job & investment scams — "easy task" jobs, crypto "opportunities," and too-good returns.
  • E-commerce scams — deals on Carousell/marketplaces where you pay and nothing arrives.
The one rule that stops most scams

No legitimate government agency, bank or police officer will ever call to ask you to transfer money, share a full OTP, or "move funds to a safe account." The moment someone pressures you to move money urgently — stop, hang up, and verify through official channels.

Other things to know

  • Drugs are treated with extreme severity — trafficking can carry the death penalty, and even consumption is heavily punished. This is not a place to take any risk. (See our guide to Singapore's laws and fines.)
  • Emergencies: Police 999, Ambulance/Fire 995, non-emergency police 1800-255-0000.
  • Report scams: the ScamShield app and the anti-scam hotline 1799 are the official channels.

Bottom line

Move around Singapore with far less street-crime anxiety than most cities — but keep your guard up online and on the phone. If you're relocating, the highest-value safety habit is learning how the rental process really works before money changes hands, because that's where newcomers lose the most.
