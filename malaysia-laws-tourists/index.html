Malaysia's rules for visitors, untangled

Malaysia confuses visitors because two legal systems run in parallel — and half the scary rules you've read apply only to one of them. Here's the untangled version.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Malaysia operates civil law for everyone alongside Islamic (Syariah) law that applies only to Muslims. Most viral "you can't do X in Malaysia" content muddles the two. As a non-Muslim visitor, your rulebook is closer to Singapore-lite — with one deadly exception.

What's completely fine for visitors

  • Alcohol — legal, sold in supermarkets, bars and restaurants (heavily taxed, so it's pricier than the food). Kelantan and Terengganu states are far more restrictive; the rest of the country is relaxed.
  • Pork — sold openly in "non-halal" sections and Chinese restaurants.
  • Normal dress — shorts and t-shirts are everywhere in KL and tourist areas; cover shoulders/knees for mosques (robes are lent) and dress modestly in conservative east-coast states.
  • Public affection — hand-holding is unremarkable; heavy PDA draws the same social frown as elsewhere in Southeast Asia, rarely anything more for tourists.

What applies only to Muslims

Khalwat (unmarried proximity), fasting obligations during Ramadan, alcohol bans, and Syariah offences generally — these headline-makers do not apply to non-Muslim visitors. During Ramadan, restaurants stay open (many put up curtains) and non-Muslims eat normally; basic daytime discretion in heavily Muslim areas is courtesy, not law.

The deadly-serious exception: drugs

Zero tolerance, capital penalties

Malaysia's Dangerous Drugs Act carries the death penalty for trafficking, with presumption clauses at surprisingly low weights — and possession of small amounts still means prison and caning. Cannabis is fully illegal (Thailand's rules stop at the border, in both directions). Never carry anything across this border for anyone.

Other real rules worth knowing

  • Vapes: regulated and in flux, with some states banning sales — bringing personal devices is currently tolerated in most states, but check current status; the trend is restrictive.
  • E-hailing is legal and excellent (Grab) — use it over unmetered taxis.
  • Jaywalking, littering: offences on paper, enforced sporadically — don't be the test case in KL's city centre.
  • Insulting religion or royalty — genuinely prosecutable, including online. Keep hot takes about the monarchy or Islam out of your posts while in-country.
