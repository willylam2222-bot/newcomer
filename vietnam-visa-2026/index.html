Getting into Vietnam — and staying: the 2026 picture

Vietnam made short stays genuinely easy with the 90-day e-visa. Long stays remain a patchwork — here's what's real, and what agents won't tell you.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Vietnam modernised its entry rules in 2023 and became one of Asia's easiest countries to visit — while remaining one of the trickier ones to stay in long-term legitimately. Both halves matter.

Short stays: genuinely easy now

  • The 90-day e-visa — available to all nationalities, single or multiple entry, ~US$25–50, applied online at the official portal (evisa.gov.vn — beware lookalike agent sites charging triple). Processing is typically under a week. This is the workhorse: three months, re-enterable, no embassy visit.
  • Visa-free entry — citizens of a set of countries (much of Western Europe, parts of Asia) get 15–45 days without any visa, and Vietnam has been progressively extending both the list and durations to boost tourism. Check your passport's current allowance; it changes.

Staying longer: the honest picture

  • Border runs still work — exit to Cambodia/Laos, re-enter on a fresh e-visa — and remain common practice. But each entry is a border officer's discretion, and Vietnam has no formal path that blesses serial tourism. It's tolerated, not guaranteed.
  • No digital-nomad visa exists yet (despite years of rumours). Nomads in Da Nang and HCMC largely live on chained e-visas — functional, but structurally precarious.
  • Real long-stay routes: work permit + TRC (temporary residence card, via a Vietnamese employer), business/investor routes (DN visas via owning a company), and family/marriage routes. All involve real paperwork and usually an agent; "visa services" that promise long-term stays without any of these are selling grey-zone workarounds that periodically get purged.
Three practical warnings

1) Use only evisa.gov.vn — the top search results are often agent sites marking up the same visa 2–3×. 2) Your e-visa dates are fixed — arriving before the start date gets you bounced. 3) Overstaying is fined per day and flagged; even one day complicates the next application. Calendar it.

The trend to watch

Vietnam keeps signalling longer visa-free windows and has openly studied nomad-visa proposals — tourism economics push toward liberalisation. Rules here genuinely change year to year, so verify at your travel date; the direction, unlike Thailand's cannabis saga, has been consistently toward easier entry.
