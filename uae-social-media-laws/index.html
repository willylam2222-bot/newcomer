The UAE's social media rules: the modern tourist trap

In most countries a rude comment is a social problem. In the UAE it can be a legal one — and this, not alcohol or dress, is the rulebook modern visitors actually trip over.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

The UAE's cybercrime framework treats online conduct — including private messages — as seriously as offline conduct, and defamation is criminal, not civil. Real cases have involved exactly the things travellers do without thinking. Here's the practical map.

What can genuinely get you in trouble

  • Photographing or filming people without consent — and especially posting them. Privacy is legally protected; a stranger identifiable in your vlog or story can file a complaint. Women and families in the background of shots are the classic case.
  • Sharing accident or crash photos/videos — explicitly an offence, even "as news."
  • Insults and swearing directed at someone — including in WhatsApp messages and voice notes. Cursing at your landlord in a text is prosecutable defamation/abuse; cases have been brought over emoji-laden rants.
  • Posting content that mocks religion, the state, or officials — treat as absolutely off-limits.
  • Filming in restricted places — government buildings, ports, military areas, some airport zones.

What's completely fine

Everything tourists mostly do: skyline shots, food, hotels, desert dunes, yourself and consenting friends, honest reviews of places (stick to facts and opinion about the service, not abuse of a person). Influencer culture thrives in Dubai — the city is arguably the world's most photographed — because the lines above are easy to stay inside once you know them.

Three posting habits that keep you clear

1) Frame shots so strangers aren't identifiable — or ask. 2) Never post accident/incident content. 3) Keep disputes offline and unemotional in writing — if you wouldn't sign it in front of a judge, don't send it.

If a dispute happens

Because insults are criminal, disputes escalate differently here: the aggrieved party files a complaint, and the case can block travel while processed. The defensive play is simple — stay coldly polite in every written exchange, however wrong the other side is, and pursue actual grievances through official channels (Dubai Police app, consumer protection) rather than public shaming posts, which can themselves be defamation.
