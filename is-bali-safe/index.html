Is Bali safe? Yes — from everything except your own scooter

Bali's crime rate is low and its hospitality is real. Its casualty list is written almost entirely by scooters, surf and moonshine — all opt-in risks.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Violent crime against tourists in Bali is rare; the island's economy runs on visitors and it shows. But Bali does injure a lot of tourists — through a specific, avoidable list. Ranked:

#1: Scooters (by a huge margin)

The classic Bali injury is a first-time rider in shorts on a rented scooter. The roads mix trucks, dogs, potholes and rain; helmets and an IDP with motorcycle category are legally required (police stops of foreigners are routine, and riding unlicensed voids most travel insurance — the same trap as Thailand and Vietnam). If you don't already ride at home, Bali is a terrible classroom: use Grab/Gojek bikes and cars instead — they're everywhere and cheap.

#2: Methanol poisoning

The arak warning

Bootleg spirits adulterated with methanol kill tourists in Indonesia — not urban legend, a recurring reality. The rule: drink sealed, branded alcohol from reputable venues; skip free-pour "arak" cocktails at rock-bottom bars, and treat suspiciously cheap spirits as the hazard they are. Beer and duty-free bottles are the safe lanes.

#3: The ocean

South-coast rip currents (Canggu through Uluwatu) are strong and beaches are inconsistently flagged/lifeguarded. Swim at flagged beaches, respect red flags absolutely, and know the rip protocol: swim parallel to shore, not against the pull. Surf injuries cluster over shallow reef breaks — know your break.

#4: Monkeys, dogs & rabies

Temple monkeys (Uluwatu, Monkey Forest) are professional thieves — sunglasses, phones, earrings — and they bite when cornered. Indonesia has rabies; any monkey or dog bite means same-day post-exposure shots, no exceptions. Don't feed, don't hold food, keep loose items zipped.

#5: The petty layer

  • Bag-snatching from bikes in Canggu/Kuta — crossbody, building side, phone off the road edge.
  • ATM skimming — use machines attached to banks; cover the PIN.
  • Party-zone pickpockets and drink-watching basics in Kuta nightlife.
  • Money changers with sleight-of-hand — use authorised changers (or just ATMs) and count on the counter.

The base layer

Dengue (repellent, daily), "Bali belly" (bottled water, high-turnover food stalls), and decent insurance — Bali's international clinics (Siloam, BIMC) are good and priced accordingly. Emergency number: 112. None of this changes the verdict: Bali is a safe island whose accidents are overwhelmingly chosen. Choose fewer of them.
