Dubai during Ramadan: the visitor's honest guide

Older guides say Dubai shuts down for a month. Modern Dubai doesn't — but the register changes, and visitors are expected to read the room.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Ramadan is the Islamic holy month of fasting from dawn to sunset. Its dates shift about 11 days earlier each year (in 2026 it fell across February–March). For visitors, Dubai during Ramadan is fully functional — hotels, malls, attractions and beaches all run — but the city's rhythm and courtesy expectations shift. Here's what that means practically.

What actually changes

  • Daytime eating & drinking in public. The old rule was strict; modern Dubai is relaxed in practice — most restaurants and cafés now serve normally through the day, some behind screens. The courtesy that remains: don't eat, drink or smoke ostentatiously in open public spaces (streets, taxis, metro) during fasting hours.
  • Hours warp. Some offices and shops shorten daytime hours, then everything comes alive after sunset — malls stay open later, and the city's real energy moves to the evening.
  • Alcohol still serves at licensed venues, though some bars dial music down and a few close daytime. Nightlife is quieter; big club events pause.
  • Dress and PDA courtesy rises a notch — cover shoulders/knees in public spaces, keep affection to zero. (See our dress & PDA guide for the baseline.)

The part guides undersell: iftar

Iftar — the sunset fast-breaking meal — is one of the best food experiences Dubai offers. Hotels and restaurants run lavish iftar spreads at very fair prices, and the communal atmosphere is genuinely special. Suhoor (the pre-dawn meal) turns into late-night dining culture. Visiting during Ramadan means eating better, not worse — just on a different clock.

The visitor's Ramadan etiquette in one list

Don't eat/drink/smoke conspicuously in public daylight · dress a notch more modestly · zero PDA · accept slower daytime service with grace · say yes to any iftar invitation · greet with "Ramadan Kareem."

Should you avoid visiting?

No — and there's a contrarian upside: hotel rates often dip, crowds thin at daytime attractions, and evenings are magical. Avoid it only if your trip is specifically about bar-hopping and club nights. Eid al-Fitr, the festival ending Ramadan, is the opposite: peak celebration, peak crowds, book ahead.
