Renting in Vietnam: the softest landing in Southeast Asia

No key money, no guarantors, deposits of a month or two, and a whole housing class built for foreigners. Renting is one of Vietnam's easiest onboarding steps.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 4 min read

Vietnam's rental market is informal, negotiable and foreigner-friendly. The serviced-apartment sector exists precisely for newcomers, and the friction points are few. Here's how it works.

The two rental lanes

  • Serviced apartments — the expat default: furnished, cleaning and laundry often included, utilities partly bundled, flexible 3–12 month terms, landlord/manager on site. Slightly pricier per square metre; dramatically lower friction. (Bands in our Vietnam cost-of-living guide.)
  • Condos and houses — more space for the money, 6–12 month contracts, bills your own. Worth it once you know your district; overkill for month one.

The standard deal

  • Deposit: 1–2 months (one is common for serviced units), plus first month upfront. No key money, no guarantor companies, no agency fee to tenants in most cases.
  • Everything negotiates — longer commitment for lower rent is standard; 10–15% movement on asking prices is normal, especially in the low season.
  • Contracts run bilingual or English; rents quote in VND or sometimes USD — pin down which, and how utilities are metered (see the electricity-rate trap below).

The two local rules that matter

  • Residence registration: your landlord must register you with the local police (a legal requirement for foreign tenants, tied to your visa/temporary residence). Good landlords do it automatically; confirm at signing — you may need proof of it for visa extensions.
  • Electricity rates: as in Thailand, some buildings resell power above the state rate. Ask the per-kWh price before signing; aircon months make the difference real.
The month-one playbook

Book an Airbnb/serviced month in the district you fancy → walk it and message building managers (Facebook groups and Zalo listings dominate) → view in person, negotiate, move in for 3–6 months. Photos flatter here; noise and light don't survive a viewing. Total setup friction: about a week.
