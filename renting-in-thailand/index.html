Renting in Thailand: easier than home, with three local quirks

Thai renting is refreshingly simple: no key money, no guarantors, agents cost you nothing. The three quirks worth knowing are deposits, TM30 and the electricity meter.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

After reading about Japanese key money or Singapore's two-month deposits, Thailand feels easy: abundant supply, furnished condos as the default, and a market that genuinely wants foreign tenants. Here's the shape of it.

The standard deal

  • Lease: 12 months standard (6 sometimes negotiable, shorter costs more); monthly serviced apartments cover short stays.
  • Upfront: typically 2 months' deposit + 1 month advance — three months' rent to move in, no other fees.
  • Agents are free for tenants — landlords pay the commission, so using one costs you nothing and they handle the paperwork. There's no exclusivity; message several.
  • Furnished is the norm for condos — bed, aircon, usually appliances. Check the inventory list against reality on day one, with photos.

Quirk #1: TM30 registration

Thai law requires your landlord to report your stay to Immigration (the TM30 filing) within 24 hours of you moving in. Why you care: you'll need proof of it (or a TM30 receipt) for visa extensions and some official processes. Good landlords file automatically; ask about it at signing rather than discovering the gap at Immigration.

Quirk #2: the electricity meter game

Government electricity rate is ~4–5 baht/unit. Some apartments — especially cheaper monthly ones — bill at 7–8+ baht/unit as a profit line. In aircon country this doubles real rent difference. Ask the rate before signing; condos on direct government meters win.

Quirk #3: getting deposits back

The classic friction point. Improve your odds: photo-document the unit at move-in, keep the inventory signed, give proper written notice, and do a walkthrough with the landlord/agent at move-out. Deduction disputes are common; documentation wins them.

Where to look

Facebook groups and agent listings dominate (Hipflat/DDproperty for browsing). For your first month in a new city, book a monthly serviced apartment, then hunt in person — photos lie about noise, light and mattress quality, and viewing five condos in a day is normal and free.
