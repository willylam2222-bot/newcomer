The AI mistakes that burn people — and the cheap fixes

AI doesn't make you look bad. Using it without a few cheap habits does — and the mistakes are the same ones, over and over.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Every viral "AI fail" — the lawyer citing fake cases, the report with "[insert data here]", the confidently wrong answer repeated to a client — traces to a short list of avoidable mistakes. Here they are, with the habit that prevents each.

1. Trusting confident hallucinations

AI invents facts, citations, statistics and quotes with total confidence. The tone is identical whether it's right or guessing. Fix: for anything checkable and consequential, verify the load-bearing facts against a real source — especially numbers, names, dates, legal/medical claims and anything recent.

2. Submitting output you didn't read

The "[COMPANY NAME]", the wrong recipient, the paragraph that contradicts itself — all from pasting AI output unread. Fix: read every word before it leaves your hands. Aloud, for anything important. Non-negotiable.

3. Pasting confidential data into public tools

The one with real consequences

Client data, unreleased financials, personal info, proprietary code — pasted into a consumer AI, it may be used for training or simply leave your control. Fix: know your tool's data policy, use enterprise/opt-out tiers for sensitive work, and never paste anything you couldn't email to a stranger. When unsure, anonymise first.

4. Relying on stale knowledge

Models have training cutoffs; laws, prices, versions and events move past them. An answer about "the latest" anything may be a year old. Fix: for time-sensitive questions, use a tool with live search or verify against a current source — and never assume the model knows today's facts.

5. Accepting the first answer

The first response is a draft, not the ceiling. People take mediocre output because they don't know it iterates. Fix: steer — "more specific", "shorter", "what are you missing", "give me a stronger version". Two rounds of iteration beats one lucky prompt.

6. Letting it flatten your judgement

Outsourcing thinking entirely — decisions, opinions, understanding — leaves you unable to defend or build on the output. Fix: use AI to inform and challenge your judgement, not replace it. Ask it to argue both sides, then decide yourself.

7. Over-agreeableness (yours and its)

Push back on a correct AI answer and weaker models cave; ask a leading question and they'll confirm your bias. Fix: ask neutrally ("is this right? argue both sides") rather than "you're wrong", and be suspicious when the AI agrees with you a little too readily.

The meta-fix

Calibrate everything to stakes: near-zero caution for brainstorms and drafts, real verification for anything involving money, health, law, reputation or other people.
