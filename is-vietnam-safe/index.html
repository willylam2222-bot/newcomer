Is Vietnam safe? Yes — once you learn to cross the street

Vietnam's dangers are almost entirely kinetic, not criminal. Master the traffic, guard the phone, and this is one of Asia's most relaxed places to travel.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Vietnam is a low-violent-crime country where solo travellers, including women, generally report feeling safe day and night. The risks that actually cost tourists are traffic, opportunistic snatching, and a short list of well-worn scams. Ranked honestly:

Risk #1: traffic — and the art of crossing

Motorbike-dense traffic is Vietnam's defining hazard, both to ride in and to walk through. If you rent a scooter: helmet always, assume nobody yields, and know that without a proper licence (IDP with motorcycle category) your travel insurance likely won't pay — the same trap as Thailand.

The street-crossing technique

Waiting for a gap doesn't work — there are no gaps. The local method: commit, walk slowly and steadily across without stopping or darting, and let the river of bikes flow around you. They're predicting your path; sudden moves break the prediction. Terrifying once, automatic by day three.

Risk #2: motorbike bag-snatching

HCMC's signature petty crime: a passing bike snatches phones and bags — from hands, café table edges, and loosely-worn shoulders. The counters are simple: crossbody straps worn on the building side, phones away from the kerb, and never film streetside with a loose grip. Hanoi sees less of it; beach towns less again.

Risk #3: the standard scams

  • Taxi games — rigged meters and lookalike cabs. Use Grab, or the two reputable meter fleets (Vinasun, Mai Linh) — and check the logo carefully; imitators mimic both.
  • The banknote shuffle — the 500,000₫ and 20,000₫ notes are both blue; "accidentally" giving change for the small one is a classic. Count deliberately; separate big notes in your wallet.
  • Friendly-vendor props — the coconut-carrier photo, the shoe-shine grab, the cyclo "agreed price" that multiplies at the end. A firm smile-and-no beats them all.

Everything else

  • Health: dengue is real (repellent, cities included); street food is largely safe via the high-turnover rule; tap water is not for drinking.
  • Nature: currents at some beaches, and north-coast typhoon season (roughly July–November) can disrupt Ha Long Bay trips.
  • Emergencies: police 113, ambulance 115. Private international clinics in HCMC/Hanoi (Family Medical, Vinmec) are the expat standard — insurance matters.

Bottom line: Vietnam asks for street smarts, not fear. The tourists who get hurt here overwhelmingly did it on a scooter; the rest is manageable with a crossbody bag and a Grab account.
