What not to do in Japan — the list that matters

Japan won't fine you for bad manners, and nobody expects perfection from visitors. But a dozen habits mark the difference between welcome guest and walking headache.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Etiquette content about Japan tends to be a 50-item anxiety list. The truth: Japanese people are patient with visitors, and most rules are absorbed by watching. These are the ones that actually matter, roughly in the order you'll meet them.

Trains (where most mistakes happen)

  • No phone calls — texting fine, calls no. The carriage is quiet; match it.
  • Queue at the platform markings, let people off first — this is real and universal.
  • Backpacks off and to your front in crowds; don't eat on commuter trains (long-distance shinkansen: bento eating is traditional and encouraged).

Food & chopsticks

  • Never stick chopsticks upright in rice, and never pass food chopstick-to-chopstick — both mimic funeral rites; they're the two genuinely loaded taboos.
  • Slurping noodles is fine (a compliment, even). Walking while eating is mildly frowned on — eat where you bought it.
  • No tipping. Ever. It causes confusion, not gratitude; great service is the default, not a transaction.

Shoes, homes and some restaurants

Shoes off at homes, temples, ryokan, some restaurants and fitting rooms — the giveaway is a genkan (sunken entrance) or a shoe rack. Slip-on shoes make your whole trip smoother. Watch for separate toilet slippers at ryokan; wearing them back into the dining room is the classic tourist blooper (locals will find it funny, not offensive).

Onsen (hot springs)

  • Wash and rinse thoroughly at the seated showers before entering the water — this is the non-negotiable one.
  • Naked means naked (swimsuits aren't allowed in traditional onsen); the small towel stays out of the bath.
  • Tattoos: traditional onsen may refuse them; many now allow small covered ones, and private (kashikiri) baths solve it entirely. Check ahead rather than testing it.
The two-word cheat code

"Sumimasen" (excuse me / sorry / thank you) covers 80% of social friction, and a small bow with it covers the rest. Effort is read generously here.

What NOT to stress about

Bowing angles (nobody expects you to know), keigo (polite Japanese), pouring rituals, business card ceremonies (tourists don't need them), and mild mistakes generally — the visitor who queues, keeps quiet on trains and washes before the onsen is already in the top tier of guests.
