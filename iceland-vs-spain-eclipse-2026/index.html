Iceland or Spain for the August 12 2026 eclipse? The honest comparison

On 12 August 2026, totality crosses both Iceland and northern Spain. Chasers are split — here's the honest trade-off, so you can pick the one that fits you.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

The 2026 eclipse gives serious viewers two very different European options on the same day. Neither is a slam dunk. The decision comes down to Sun height, weather risk, crowds and logistics.

Iceland: higher Sun, wilder weather

  • Sun height: considerably higher above the horizon than in Spain — a genuine advantage, since obstruction and horizon haze are less of a threat.
  • Weather: the catch. Iceland in August is famously changeable and cloudy; clear skies at the right moment are a real gamble, and the island is small so escaping cloud is hard.
  • Logistics & cost: limited accommodation, high prices, and everyone chasing the same few clear-ish spots. Book far ahead.

Spain: low Sun, better odds, easier trip

  • Sun height: the drawback — only 2–12° above the horizon, so you must have a clear western view (see our where-to-stand guide).
  • Weather: the interior Meseta has genuinely good August clear-sky odds, and the country is big enough to drive to clearer sky.
  • Logistics & cost: far more beds, roads and options; warm summer weather; easier to reach and cheaper if you base smart.

How to choose

Pick Iceland if…you want the highest, most dramatic Sun, you're an experienced chaser comfortable gambling on cloud, and you'll book early and pay up.
Pick Spain if…you want better weather odds, a mobile Plan B, warmth, easier logistics and lower cost — and you're willing to secure a clear western horizon.
The pragmatic verdict: for most travellers, Spain's better clear-sky odds and drive-to-clear-sky flexibility beat Iceland's higher Sun but riskier weather — provided you solve the low-horizon problem. That's exactly what the Spain playbook is built around.
