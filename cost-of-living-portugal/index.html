What Portugal actually costs now — Lisbon isn't the bargain it was

Portugal's food, transport and healthcare remain wonderfully cheap for Western Europe. Its big-city housing no longer is — and pretending otherwise sets newcomers up badly.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

A decade of expat influx, golden-visa money and tourism turned Lisbon from Europe's cheap capital into a mid-priced one — while the rest of daily life stayed remarkably affordable. The honest picture is that split.

  • Landlords commonly ask 2–3 months upfront (deposit + advance), and increasingly for proof of income or a guarantor; the competitive Lisbon market moves in days.
  • The value plays: Porto (80% of the charm, 70% of the price), the Setúbal/Almada side of the river, or the smaller university cities if remote work frees you from Lisbon.

Where Portugal is still gloriously cheap

  • The menu do dia — a full lunch (soup, main, drink, coffee) at neighbourhood tascas for €10–15, still one of Western Europe's best daily deals.
  • Coffee — €0.80–1.30 for an espresso at the counter; the €4 flat white is an expat-café invention, not a Portuguese price.
  • Transport — metro/bus passes around €40/month in Lisbon; intercity trains cheap by EU standards.
  • Healthcare — the public SNS is accessible to legal residents (modest fees), and private insurance runs €40–100/month — a fraction of US-style pricing for high-quality care.
  • Groceries, wine (excellent at €4–8), and utilities (€100–180/month with gas and net) all sit below Western-EU norms.
Honest monthly budgets (single person)

~€1,400: room in a Lisbon shared flat or solo in a smaller city, local eating — the realistic entry point. ~€2,200–2,800: own 1BR in Lisbon/Porto, mixed lifestyle — the standard nomad/professional number (note how the D8 income bar of ~€3,500 maps onto this with margin). Families: housing + international school (€6–15k/year, far below Asia's expat schools) makes Portugal one of the cheaper Western family relocations.
