How foreigners actually open a Singapore bank account

With a valid pass it's easy. Before your pass is approved, it's mostly not — here's the honest sequence, and the bridge to use in between.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

Bank marketing makes this look effortless; the reality depends entirely on your immigration status. Here's how it actually goes.

If you hold a pass (EP, S Pass, Student Pass, DP)

Straightforward. The big three local banks — DBS/POSB, OCBC, UOB — all open accounts for pass holders, often fully online. You'll typically need:

  • Passport
  • Your pass (or the In-Principle Approval letter — some banks accept the IPA before the physical card)
  • Proof of local address (tenancy agreement, or a letter/bill; some banks are flexible early on)

Digital banks (e.g. Trust, MariBank, GXS) are also easy adds later, but your salary account is usually best at one of the big three — employers, landlords and PayNow all assume it.

Before your pass is approved

This is the gap that catches newcomers: most local banks won't open a resident account on just a visitor's visa (some offer premium accounts with large minimum deposits, but that's not most people). The practical bridge:

  • A multi-currency card from home — Wise, Revolut or similar — so you can pay deposits and daily costs with low FX fees while you wait.
  • Open the local account in week one or two after your pass/IPA is in hand, then switch your salary and rent payments to it.
Set up PayNow immediately

Once your account is open, register PayNow (linked to your mobile/FIN). It's how Singapore actually pays — hawkers, landlords, splitting bills. Without it you'll feel locked out of daily life.

Traps to avoid

  • Fall-below fees — many accounts charge a monthly fee if your balance dips under a minimum (often S$1,000–3,000). Check before choosing.
  • Opening "any" account at the airport-style sales booth — compare the basic savings accounts of the big three first; they differ mostly on fall-below minimums and card perks.
  • Scam warnings are real: banks will never call asking you to move money. From day one, treat any such call as a scam (see our scams guide).
