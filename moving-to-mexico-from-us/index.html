Moving to Mexico from the US: what actually to do, in order

Mexico is the #1 destination for Americans moving abroad — close, cheap, sunny. The mistakes are predictable, and mostly about the things that follow you across the border.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

Proximity makes moving to Mexico feel casual — a flight, not an expedition. That casualness is the trap: the things that bite Americans are the boring ones (taxes, healthcare, banking) they assumed would sort themselves out. The practical sequence:

The thing nobody escapes: US taxes

The US taxes citizens worldwide

Unlike almost every other country, the US taxes its citizens on global income no matter where you live. Moving to Mexico does not end your US filing obligation. You'll likely owe little via the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion or Foreign Tax Credit — but you must still file, and you must report foreign bank accounts (FBAR) over $10k. Also become a Mexican tax resident after ~183 days and you may owe Mexican tax too. Talk to a cross-border accountant before, not after — this is the expensive mistake.

Healthcare: cheaper, and you'll pay out of pocket

  • Mexican private healthcare is excellent and a fraction of US prices — many Americans simply pay cash for routine care (a specialist visit $30–50).
  • US Medicare does NOT cover you in Mexico. Options: private Mexican insurance ($50–150/month), an international plan, or self-insuring for routine + evacuation cover for emergencies. Don't arrive uninsured assuming "it's cheap."

The setup chain (first 90 days)

  • Residency first if staying — apply at a US consulate before you move (see our visa guide); it unlocks everything else legally.
  • Banking: opening a Mexican bank account needs residency; until then, use a US card with no foreign fees + a service like Wise for peso transfers. Keep a US account and address (many US banks close accounts with foreign addresses — use a family address or mail service).
  • Phone: a Telcel SIM on arrival; cheap and best coverage.
  • Driving: your US license works as a tourist; residents should get a Mexican license (straightforward). Importing a US car (the "TIP" temporary import permit) is a bureaucratic saga — many sell the US car and buy or go carless in CDMX.
  • Pets: dogs and cats enter easily with recent vet paperwork — one of the genuinely simple parts.

The culture shifts Americans underestimate

  • Bureaucracy runs on patience and paper — originals, copies, stamps, and a different relationship with time. Fighting it raises your blood pressure and changes nothing.
  • Spanish matters more than expat forums admit — CDMX has English-speaking bubbles, but life, paperwork and real friendship open up fast with even basic Spanish. Start before you go.
  • You're a guest in a real country, not a lifestyle backdrop — the gentrification tension is real (see our cost-of-living note). Learning the language, tipping well, and not treating neighborhoods as content goes a long way.
