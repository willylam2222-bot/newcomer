Healthcare in Singapore when you're not a citizen

Singapore healthcare is world-class and fast. It is also, for foreigners, entirely out-of-pocket unless you're insured — and hospital numbers get big quickly.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 5 min read

The quality story is simple: excellent doctors, short waits, English everywhere. The money story is what newcomers get wrong — the national safety nets (MediShield Life, subsidised public rates) are for citizens and PRs. As a foreigner, you pay the unsubsidised rate, or your insurance does.

What things cost (indicative, out of pocket)

CareRough cost
GP visit at a neighbourhood clinic (with basic meds)~S$30–70
Specialist consultation (private)~S$150–300+
A&E attendance~S$150–250 before treatment
Hospital ward per night (private)Several hundred to S$1,000+
Surgery + stayFive figures fast

Day-to-day illness is affordable — see a GP, pay S$40, done. The risk you're managing is the rare event: an accident or serious diagnosis where bills reach tens of thousands.

So do you need insurance? Yes — here's the hierarchy

  • Employed on an EP/S Pass: check your employment contract first — many employers provide group health cover. Read what it actually includes (outpatient? hospitalisation only? dental?).
  • S Pass and Work Permit holders: employers are legally required to provide medical insurance at a mandated minimum — but the minimum is basic; topping up is sensible.
  • Students: universities typically bundle a basic plan; check its hospitalisation cap.
  • Dependants/self-employed: you're on your own — buy an expat or local integrated plan before you need it.
The one-line rule

Insure for hospitalisation first — that's the tail risk that ruins finances. Outpatient GP visits you can afford in cash; a week in intensive care you cannot.

Practical notes

  • Pharmacies (Guardian, Watsons, Unity) handle everyday needs; pharmacists can advise on minor ailments.
  • Public hospitals treat foreigners — you just pay unsubsidised rates. For emergencies, go to the nearest A&E regardless; sort billing later.
  • Bring a supply of your regular prescription meds and transition to a local GP early (see our medication guide for what needs HSA approval).
