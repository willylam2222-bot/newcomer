Spain's total eclipse, August 12 2026: where to stand, when to book

On 12 August 2026, a total solar eclipse crosses northern Spain — the first from mainland Europe since 1999. Here's where totality actually falls, and the one catch almost every guide misses.

By the Newcomer editorial team · Last verified July 2026 · 6 min read

For under two minutes on 12 August 2026, the Moon fully covers the Sun over a band of northern Spain — the continent's biggest astronomy event in a generation. Hotels in the path are already several times normal price and selling out. Before you book anything, understand where totality falls and the catch that decides whether you actually see it.

Who's in the path — and who isn't

  • The band of totality runs coast to coast across the north: A Coruña and Galicia on the Atlantic, through Oviedo, León, Palencia, Burgos and Zaragoza, out to Valencia and Palma de Mallorca on the Mediterranean.
  • Madrid and Barcelona are OUT. Both sit just outside the path and see only a partial eclipse — do not plan your trip around them.
  • Maximum totality is only about 1 minute 48 seconds near the centre-line (Oviedo/León).
The catch that ruins most views: this is a low-sun eclipse. At totality the Sun sits just 2° above the horizon in the Balearics and only ~12° in Galicia — minutes before sunset. A hill, a building or a treeline to your west-northwest can hide the entire event. A "great hotel in a totality town" is worthless if its western sky is blocked.

Where to actually stand

  • Face west-northwest (roughly 290–300°) and stand where that direction is open sky.
  • Gain a little elevation — a low hill, a west-facing slope, a rooftop or a beach looking over water beats a valley floor.
  • Water or flat plains to the west are ideal — Atlantic beaches in Galicia, or the open Meseta.
  • Scout the terrain west of your viewpoint on a map before you book, not on the day.

The towns, quickly ranked

Castile y León belt (Salamanca→Zamora→Valladolid→Palencia)The sweet spot: centre-line-ish totality, flat open horizon, the region's best clear-sky odds, quieter roads.
A Coruña / GaliciaHighest Sun (~12°) so best against the horizon, but Atlantic cloud and sea haze are a real risk.
ZaragozaBig city, good transport, near the centre-line; low sun (~6°).
Valencia / PalmaSun almost setting (2–4°) — dramatic, but you need a perfectly clean sea horizon.

Clear-sky odds and a Plan B

The dry interior Meseta (Castile y León, Aragón) has the best statistical clear-sky odds in August; Galicia and the coasts are riskier. Because low sun means even thin horizon cloud can block the view, stay mobile if you can — a base with a car lets you drive along the path to clearer sky on the morning of the 12th. Rigid, non-refundable, no-car plans are the biggest avoidable risk.

Booking before it sells out

  • Prices in centre-line towns are already 5–10× normal and packages are selling out. Book now.
  • Use the base-and-drive approach: book a bed in a larger, cheaper city off the hot centre and drive to a scouted west-open viewpoint on eclipse day.
  • Rent the car early — vehicles near the path vanish before rooms do. Book refundable where possible.

Eye safety, in one line

Wear ISO-12312-2 eclipse glasses for the entire partial phase; the naked eye is only safe during full totality, and glasses go back on the instant the Sun's edge reappears. Cameras and phones need a solar filter during the partial phase.
